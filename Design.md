# Design Guidelines

## 1. Design Vision
Syllabot should feel calm, clear, and reassuring. The product should reduce exam anxiety rather than intensify it. The design language should make planning feel structured, supportive, and manageable.

## 2. Core UX Principles
- Clarity over complexity
- Calmness over urgency
- Trust and confidence over stimulation
- Progress visibility over pressure
- Minimal friction for daily check-ins

## 3. Visual Direction
### Tone
The experience should feel like a thoughtful academic companion rather than a harsh productivity tool. It should help students focus on the next small step, not overwhelm them with noise.

### Style Preferences
- Clean, spacious layouts
- Soft but confident interface hierarchy
- Focused cards and panels for subjects, tasks, and progress
- Gentle motion and transitions
- Strong readability for long study sessions

## 4. Color Palette
The palette should emphasize stability, trust, and progress.

### Primary Colors
- Deep trust blue: `#1F4E79`
- Slate navy: `#23395B`
- Calm academic blue: `#4E7DA0`

### Accent Colors
- Progress teal: `#2E9D9A`
- Soft mint: `#7BC8B7`
- Warm neutral: `#F5F7FA`

### Supporting Neutrals
- Off-white: `#FAFBFD`
- Light gray: `#E8EEF3`
- Charcoal: `#2F3B4C`

These colors are intended to reduce exam anxiety by creating a feeling of order, steadiness, and forward momentum.

## 5. Typography
### Recommended Type System
- Primary font: Inter
- Secondary font: Source Sans 3 or Manrope
- Monospace font: JetBrains Mono for technical or structured content only

### Typography Principles
- Use generous line-height for readability
- Keep headings calm and structured
- Favor strong contrast without harshness
- Avoid overly playful or distracting type treatments

## 6. Layout and Interaction Design
### Layout
- A simple left-to-right or top-to-bottom information flow
- Clear sections for: syllabus input, current plan, progress, and next steps
- Enough whitespace to reduce cognitive load

### Interaction Patterns
- Daily check-ins should be fast and low-friction
- Plan adjustments should be visually explained rather than silently applied
- Progress should be represented through gentle indicators such as completed blocks, streaks, or milestone states

## 7. Component Design Guidance
- Cards should feel light, structured, and easy to scan
- Buttons should be clear and purposeful, not overloaded
- Empty states should be encouraging and actionable
- Error states should be calm and helpful, not alarming

## 8. Accessibility and Inclusivity
- Ensure strong contrast and readable typography
- Provide keyboard-friendly navigation
- Avoid color-only communication for status changes
- Support screen-reader-friendly labels and clear structure

## 9. Design Outcomes
The final product should make students feel:
- more in control
- less overwhelmed
- more aware of their progress
- more likely to continue studying consistently
